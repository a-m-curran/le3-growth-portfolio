Week 1 Discussion Post: Organizational Structure at My Workplace

I work part-time at a mid-sized nonprofit that runs after-school programs in three different cities. On paper, the org chart is pretty flat — there's an executive director, three program directors (one per city), and then site coordinators like me. But in practice, the structure feels way more hierarchical than it looks.

Everything has to go through the program director before anything changes. Last month I noticed that our attendance tracking system was creating duplicate entries, which meant our grant reports were inflated. I flagged it to my program director, who said she'd "look into it." Two weeks later, nothing had happened. I asked again and she said she'd brought it up to the ED but hadn't heard back.

What frustrated me was that I could have fixed it myself in about 20 minutes — it was literally a Google Sheets formula issue. But the chain of command meant I needed permission to edit a shared document that I use every single day. The formal structure created a bottleneck that slowed down a simple fix by weeks.

This connects to what we learned about mechanistic vs. organic organizational designs. My nonprofit talks about being collaborative and flexible (organic), but the actual decision-making process is very mechanistic — centralized authority, rigid role definitions, and top-down communication. The mission statement says "we empower our team," but the structure doesn't match.

I think this is what happens when organizations grow without intentionally redesigning their structure. When the org was just the ED and a few people, informal communication worked fine. But as it grew to 30+ staff across three cities, those informal norms calcified into a hierarchy that nobody consciously chose.

The Organisational Structures video helped me see that there isn't one "right" structure — it depends on what the organization needs. But I think my org needs to at least acknowledge the gap between how they describe themselves and how they actually operate.
